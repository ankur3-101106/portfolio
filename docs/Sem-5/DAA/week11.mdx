## Lecture 51: <br />Cryptoanalysis and Stream Cipher
:::

## Stream Cipher (LFSR Model)

* Keystream:
  $$
  z_t = f(s_t)
  $$

* Encryption:
  $$
  c_t = p_t \oplus z_t
  $$

* Decryption:
  $$
  p_t = c_t \oplus z_t
  $$

---

## LFSR Structure

* State:
  $$
  s_t
  $$

* Linear recurrence:
  $$
  s_{t+1} = \sum a_i s_{t-i}
  $$

✔ Defined by feedback polynomial

---

## Problem

* LFSR is **linear**
  ❌ Easily breakable

---

## Solution

* Add **non-linear function**:
  $$
  z_t = f(s_t)
  $$

✔ Improves security

---

## Algebraic Attack

---

## Idea

* From:
  $$
  z_t = c_t \oplus p_t
  $$

* If plaintext known:
  $$
  z_t \text{ known}
  $$

---

* Also:
  $$
  z_t = f(s_t)
  $$

---

## Build Equations

* For multiple time steps:
  $$
  f(s_t) = z_t
  $$

✔ System of equations

---

## Goal

* Solve for:
  $$
  s_t \Rightarrow \text{recover key}
  $$

---

## Problem

* Equations are **non-linear**

---

## Linearization

* Replace products:

$$
x_i x_j = y_{ij}
$$

---

### Example

$$
x_1 x_2 \rightarrow y_{12}
$$

$$
x_1 x_3 \rightarrow y_{13}
$$

---

## Result

* Convert system into:
  $$
  \text{linear equations in } y_{ij}
  $$

✔ Easier to solve

---

## Re-linearization

* Use identities like:

$$
x_1 x_2 x_3 x_4
$$

* Rewrite as combinations:

$$
(x_1 x_2)(x_3 x_4)
$$

* Substitute:
  $$
  y_{12},; y_{34}
  $$

---

## Key Insight

* Non-linear → transformed into linear system
* Solve using linear algebra

---

## Limitation

* Number of variables increases rapidly
* Needs enough equations

---

## Final Summary

* LFSR → linear → weak
* Add non-linearity → stronger
* Algebraic attack:

  * Build equations
  * Linearize
  * Solve system

✔ Recovers internal state / key

## Lecture 52: <br />Modern Stream Ciphers
:::

## Modern Stream Cipher Structure

---

## Basic Working

* Keystream:
  $$
  z_t
  $$

* Encryption:
  $$
  c_t = p_t \oplus z_t
  $$

---

## Initialization

* Key + IV → initial state:
  $$
  s_0
  $$

* Run cipher for some rounds (warm-up)

✔ Removes initial bias

---

## LFSR + Non-linearity

* Linear part:
  $$
  \text{LFSR}
  $$

* Non-linear part:
  $$
  \text{NLFSR / Boolean functions}
  $$

✔ Combined for security

---

## General Model

* State:
  $$
  s_t
  $$

* Output:
  $$
  z_t = h(s_t)
  $$

* Update:
  $$
  s_{t+1} = g(s_t)
  $$

---

## Key Idea

* LFSR → fast but linear
* Add:

  * Non-linear function (g)
  * Non-linear output (h)

✔ Prevents algebraic attacks

---

## Example Structure (Typical)

* LFSR generates bits
* NLFSR mixes bits
* Output function combines both

---

## SNOW-like Design

* Uses:

  * LFSR (linear state update)
  * FSM (non-linear mixing)

* Operates in:
  $$
  GF(2^8)
  $$

* Uses polynomial-based arithmetic

---

## Grain Cipher (Example)

---

### Components

* LFSR (linear)
* NLFSR (non-linear)

---

### Output

$$
z_t = h(x_1, x_2, ..., x_n)
$$

---

### Initialization

* Run:
  $$
  256 \text{ cycles}
  $$

✔ Before generating keystream

---

## Functions

* Feedback:
  $$
  g(\cdot)
  $$

* Output:
  $$
  h(\cdot)
  $$

✔ Both include non-linearity

---

## Important Concepts

### 1. Internal State

$$
s_t
$$

* Determines entire keystream

---

### 2. Key Recovery Target

* Attacker tries to find:
  $$
  s_0 \text{ or key}
  $$

---

### 3. Security

* Depends on:

  * Non-linearity
  * State size
  * Initialization

---

## Attacks

* Algebraic attacks
* Correlation attacks
* Known keystream attacks

---

## Weakness

* Poor design → recover:
  $$
  s_t
  $$

→ Full keystream known

---

## Example Cipher

* Helix (stream cipher design)

---

## Summary

* Modern stream ciphers:

  * LFSR + NLFSR
  * Non-linear output
  * Initialization phase

✔ Designed to resist linear & algebraic attacks

## Lecture 53: <br />Shamir secret sharing 
:::

## Secret Sharing (Threshold Scheme)

---

## Goal

* Split secret:
  $$
  S
  $$

* Into shares so that:

  * Any:
    $$
    t \text{ users}
    $$
    → can recover

  * Less than (t)
    → cannot learn anything

---

## Polynomial Construction

* Choose random polynomial:
  $$
  f(x) = S + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \cdots + a_{t-1} x^{t-1}
  $$

* Degree:
  $$
  t-1
  $$

---

## Share Distribution

* For each user (i):

$$
\text{Share}_i = (i,; f(i))
$$

---

## Reconstruction (Lagrange Interpolation)

* Using any (t) shares:

$$
S = f(0)
$$

---

### Formula

$$
f(0) = \sum_{i=1}^{t} y_i \cdot \lambda_i
$$

where

$$
\lambda_i = \prod_{j \ne i} \frac{x_j}{x_j - x_i}
$$

---

## Key Property

* Polynomial of degree (t-1)
  → uniquely determined by (t) points

✔ Security guarantee

---

## Security Insight

* With fewer than (t) shares:

$$
\text{Infinite possible polynomials}
$$

❌ Secret cannot be guessed

---

## Visual Intuition

* 1 share → many curves possible
* (t) shares → single curve

✔ Secret fixed

---

## Application: Group Key Recovery

* Secret key:
  $$
  K = f(0)
  $$

* Each user has:
  $$
  f(i)
  $$

* Any (t) users:

  * Combine shares
  * Recover:
    $$
    K
    $$

---

## Broadcast Scenario

* Authority (GM) generates:
  $$
  f(x)
  $$

* Sends shares to users

* Broadcasts helper function:
  $$
  h(x) = f(x) + r(x)
  $$

---

## User Computation

* Each user knows:
  $$
  f(i)
  $$

* Using broadcast:
  $$
  h(i)
  $$

* Can compute:
  $$
  r(i)
  $$

---

## Key Insight

* Only authorized group (≥ t users):

  * Can reconstruct secret

---

## Advantages

* Fault tolerance
* No single point of failure
* Secure distributed key management

---

## Summary

* Secret → polynomial
* Shares → points on curve
* Reconstruction → interpolation

✔ Foundation of secure group cryptography

## Lecture 54: <br />Identity based Encryption (IBE)
:::

## Identity-Based Encryption (IBE)

---

## Idea

* Public key = **user identity**
  $$
  ID \in {0,1}^*
  $$

✔ No certificates needed

---

## Setup

* Choose groups:
  $$
  G,; G_T
  $$

* Generator:
  $$
  g \in G
  $$

* Master secret:
  $$
  \alpha
  $$

* Public key:
  $$
  g^\alpha
  $$

---

## Hash Functions

* Map identity:
  $$
  H_1(ID) \in G
  $$

* Key derivation:
  $$
  H_2: G_T \rightarrow {0,1}^\ell
  $$

---

## Bilinear Pairing

$$
e: G \times G \rightarrow G_T
$$

---

### Properties

1. Bilinearity:
   $$
   e(u^a,; v^b) = e(u,v)^{ab}
   $$

2. Non-degenerate:
   $$
   e(g,g) \ne 1
   $$

3. Efficient

---

## Private Key Generation

* For user with identity (ID):

$$
d_{ID} = H_1(ID)^\alpha
$$

---

## Encryption

* Message:
  $$
  m
  $$

* Choose random:
  $$
  r
  $$

---

### Compute

$$
U = g^r
$$

$$
V = m \oplus H_2\big(e(H_1(ID),; g^\alpha)^r\big)
$$

---

## Ciphertext

$$
C = (U,; V)
$$

---

## Decryption

* Using private key:
  $$
  d_{ID}
  $$

---

### Compute

$$
e(d_{ID},; U) = e(H_1(ID)^\alpha,; g^r)
$$

$$
= e(H_1(ID),; g)^{\alpha r}
$$

---

### Recover Message

$$
m = V \oplus H_2\big(e(d_{ID},; U)\big)
$$

---

## Correctness

* Encryption uses:
  $$
  e(H_1(ID),; g^\alpha)^r
  $$

* Decryption computes same:
  $$
  e(H_1(ID),; g)^{\alpha r}
  $$

✔ Values match

---

## Key Insight

* Identity → public key
* Trusted authority generates private keys

---

## Advantages

* No certificates
* Simple key management

---

## Limitation

* Key escrow problem:

  * Authority knows all private keys

---

## Summary

* Uses:

  * Pairing (e)
  * Hash to group
  * XOR masking

✔ Foundation: Boneh–Franklin IBE scheme

## Lecture 55: <br />Attribute based Encryption (ABE)
:::

## Attribute-Based Encryption (ABE)

---

## Idea

* Instead of identity → use **attributes**

$$
\text{Attributes} = {\text{MSc},; \text{Math},; \text{Student},; ...}
$$

✔ Access based on properties, not identity

---

## Motivation

* Traditional (IBE):

  * One-to-one communication

* ABE:

  * One-to-many communication

✔ Encrypt once → many authorized users can decrypt

---

## Access Policy

* Ciphertext defined by policy:

$$
\text{Policy } = (\text{MSc} \land \text{Math})
$$

* Only users with matching attributes can decrypt

---

## Types of ABE

### 1. Key-Policy ABE (KP-ABE)

* Ciphertext → attributes
* Key → policy

---

### 2. Ciphertext-Policy ABE (CP-ABE)

* Ciphertext → policy
* Key → attributes

✔ More practical

---

## Setup

* Groups:
  $$
  G,; G_T
  $$

* Generator:
  $$
  g
  $$

* Pairing:
  $$
  e: G \times G \rightarrow G_T
  $$

---

## Encryption

* Message:
  $$
  m
  $$

* Random:
  $$
  r
  $$

---

### Compute

$$
C_1 = g^r
$$

$$
C_2 = m \oplus H_2\big(e(H_1(\text{attributes}),; g^\alpha)^r\big)
$$

---

## Ciphertext

$$
C = (C_1,; C_2,; \text{policy})
$$

---

## Key Generation

* For user with attributes:
  $$
  \text{Attr}_u
  $$

* Generate secret key components

---

## Decryption

* If attributes satisfy policy:

---

### Compute pairing

$$
e(d,; C_1)
$$

---

### Recover

$$
m = C_2 \oplus H_2(\text{pairing result})
$$

---

## Key Insight

* Uses bilinear property:

$$
e(g^a,; g^b) = e(g,g)^{ab}
$$

✔ Enables policy-based access

---

## Example

* Policy:

  * “MSc Math Students”

* Users:

  * MSc Math → ✔ decrypt
  * BSc Math → ❌ cannot
  * MSc Physics → ❌ cannot

---

## Applications

* Secure file sharing
* Cloud access control
* Broadcast encryption

---

## Broadcast Scenario

* Encrypt once
* Only authorized subset:
  $$
  S
  $$
  can decrypt

---

## Advantages

* Fine-grained access control
* No need for multiple encryptions

---

## Limitation

* Complex key management
* Computation overhead

---

## Summary

* Identity → replaced by attributes
* Policy-based encryption
* Built using:

  * Pairings
  * Hash functions

✔ Powerful for real-world access control systems
