## Lecture 26: <br />More on RSA
:::

## Fast Modular Exponentiation (Square & Multiply)

### Goal

Compute:
$$
a^n \mod m
$$

efficiently

---

### Idea

* Write exponent in **binary**:
  $$
  n = (b_k b_{k-1} ... b_0)_2
  $$

* Then:
  $$
  a^n = a^{b_0 2^0} \cdot a^{b_1 2^1} \cdots a^{b_k 2^k}
  $$

---

### Key Observation

$$
a^{2^i} = (a^{2^{i-1}})^2
$$

✔ Compute using repeated squaring

---

### Complexity

* Number of steps:
  $$
  O(\log n)
  $$

✔ Much faster than naive ( O(n) )

---

## Algorithm (Concept)

1. Convert ( n ) to binary
2. Initialize result = 1
3. For each bit:

   * Square current value
   * If bit = 1 → multiply

---

## Side-Channel Attack (Timing Attack)

### Problem

* Computation time depends on:

  * Bits of exponent

---

### Idea

* Attacker measures:

  * Execution time
* Infers:

  * Whether bit = 1 or 0

---

### Example Insight

* If extra multiplication occurs → bit = 1
* Leads to **key leakage**

---

## RSA Key Generation (Practical Issues)

---

### 1. Prime Generation

Need:
$$
p, q \text{ large primes (e.g., 512+ bits)}
$$

---

### Naive Method (Inefficient)

* Check divisibility up to:
  $$
  \sqrt{n}
  $$

❌ Too slow for large numbers

---

### Sieve Method

* Remove multiples:

  * 2, 3, 5, ...

✔ Works for small ( n )
❌ Not practical for large primes

---

### Efficient Approach

* Use **probabilistic tests**

#### Example:

* Miller-Rabin test

✔ Fast
✔ High probability of correctness

---

## RSA Security

### Based On

$$
n = p \cdot q
$$

* Public:
  $$
  n
  $$

* Secret:
  $$
  p, q
  $$

---

### Hard Problem

$$
\text{Factorization of large integers}
$$

---

### Why Secure?

* Given:
  $$
  n
  $$

❌ Hard to compute:
$$
p, q
$$

---

## Key Insight

* If attacker finds:
  $$
  p, q
  $$

→ Can compute:
$$
\phi(n)
$$

→ Then:
$$
d
$$

→ System breaks

---

## Key Takeaways

* Square & multiply:

  * Efficient exponentiation
* Timing attacks:

  * Real-world vulnerability
* Prime generation:

  * Uses probabilistic tests
* RSA security:

  * Depends on factorization hardness

---

## Final Conclusion

* RSA efficiency:
  $$
  O(\log n)
  $$

* RSA security:
  $$
  \text{Hardness of factoring } n = p \cdot q
  $$

✔ Still widely used, but requires:

* Large keys
* Secure implementations

## Lecture 27: <br />Primality Testing
:::
## Goal

* Check whether a number ( n ) is **prime or composite**

---

## Fermat Primality Test

### Idea (from Fermat’s Little Theorem)

If ( p ) is prime and ( \gcd(a,p)=1 ), then:

$$
a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}
$$

---

## Algorithm

1. Choose random:
   $$
   a < n
   $$

2. Compute:
   $$
   y = a^{n-1} \mod n
   $$

3. Check:

* If:
  $$
  y \ne 1
  $$
  → ( n ) is **composite** ✅

* If:
  $$
  y = 1
  $$
  → ( n ) is **probably prime**

---

## Limitation

* Some composite numbers pass test
* Called:

  * **Fermat pseudoprimes**

---

## Miller–Rabin Primality Test

### Stronger Test

---

## Step 1: Write

$$
n - 1 = 2^k \cdot m
$$

where:
$$
m \text{ is odd}
$$

---

## Step 2: Choose Random Base

$$
a < n
$$

---

## Step 3: Compute

$$
b_0 = a^m \mod n
$$

---

## Step 4: Check Conditions

* If:
  $$
  b_0 \equiv 1 \pmod{n}
  $$
  → probably prime

---

* Else compute:

$$
b_i = b_{i-1}^2 \mod n
$$

Check if any:
$$
b_i \equiv -1 \pmod{n}
$$

→ probably prime

---

## Final Decision

* If none satisfy:
  $$
  \pm 1
  $$

→ ( n ) is **composite** ✅

---

## Key Property

* If test says **composite** → always correct
* If says **prime** → probabilistic

---

## Why Better than Fermat

* Detects:

  * Carmichael numbers
* Much lower error probability

---

## Key Takeaways

* Fermat test:

  * Fast but weak
* Miller-Rabin:

  * Fast and reliable
* Used in:

  * RSA key generation

---

## Final Conclusion

* Primality testing is:
  $$
  \text{Essential for generating large primes}
  $$

* Practical systems use:

  * **Miller-Rabin test**

✔ Efficient + high confidence for cryptography

## Lecture 28: <br />ElGamal Cryptosystem.
:::

## Overview

* Public key cryptosystem based on:
  $$
  \text{Discrete Logarithm Problem}
  $$


---

## Setup (Bob)

### Step 1: Choose Parameters

* Prime:
  $$
  p
  $$

* Generator:
  $$
  \alpha \in \mathbb{Z}_p^*
  $$

---

### Step 2: Choose Secret Key

$$
a \in {1,2,...,p-2}
$$

---

### Step 3: Compute Public Value

$$
\beta = \alpha^a \mod p
$$

---

## Keys

* Public key:
  $$
  (p, \alpha, \beta)
  $$

* Private key:
  $$
  a
  $$

---

## Encryption (Alice)

### Input

* Message:
  $$
  x \in \mathbb{Z}_p^*
  $$

* Choose random:
  $$
  k
  $$

---

### Compute Ciphertext

$$
y_1 = \alpha^k \mod p
$$

$$
y_2 = x \cdot \beta^k \mod p
$$

---

### Ciphertext

$$
(y_1, y_2)
$$

---

## Decryption (Bob)

### Step 1

$$
y_1^a = (\alpha^k)^a = \alpha^{ak} = \beta^k \mod p
$$

---

### Step 2

$$
x = y_2 \cdot (y_1^a)^{-1} \mod p
$$

---

## Why It Works

$$
y_2 = x \cdot \beta^k
$$

$$
y_1^a = \beta^k
$$

So:

$$
x = \frac{y_2}{\beta^k} \mod p
$$

✔ Correct decryption

---

## Important Property

* Same message → different ciphertexts

✔ Because of random:
$$
k
$$

---

## Example

### Given

$$
p = 2579,; \alpha = 2,; a = 765
$$

---

### Compute

$$
\beta = 2^{765} \mod 2579 = 949
$$

---

### Encryption

Message:
$$
x = 1299
$$

Choose:
$$
k
$$

---

$$
y_1 = 2^k \mod 2579 = 435
$$

$$
y_2 = 1299 \cdot 949^k \mod 2579 = 2396
$$

---

### Ciphertext

$$
(435, 2396)
$$

---

## Decryption

$$
x = y_2 \cdot (y_1^a)^{-1} \mod p
$$

$$
x = 1299
$$

✔ Original message recovered

---

## Security

* Based on:
  $$
  \text{Discrete logarithm hardness}
  $$

---

## Key Takeaways

* Uses:

  * Modular exponentiation
* Randomness:

  * Provides semantic security
* Different from RSA:

  * Non-deterministic

---

## Final Conclusion

* ElGamal provides:

  * Strong security
  * Randomized encryption

$$
\text{Widely used in cryptographic protocols and digital signatures}
$$

## Lecture 29: <br />Elliptic Curve over the Reals
:::

## Curve Equation

$$
y^2 = x^3 + ax + b
$$

* Condition:
  $$
  4a^3 + 27b^2 \ne 0
  $$

✔ Ensures no singular points 

---

## Point Set

* Points:
  $$
  (x,y)
  $$
  satisfying equation

* Plus **point at infinity**:
  $$
  \mathcal{O}
  $$

---

## Point Addition

Let:

* $P = (x_1, y_1)$
* $Q = (x_2, y_2)$

---

### Case 1: $P \ne Q$

Slope:
$$
\lambda = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}
$$

Coordinates:

$$
x_3 = \lambda^2 - x_1 - x_2
$$

$$
y_3 = \lambda(x_1 - x_3) - y_1
$$

---

### Case 2: $Q = -P$

$$
P + Q = \mathcal{O}
$$

* Where:
  $$
  (x, y) \rightarrow (x, -y)
  $$

---

### Case 3: $P = Q$ (Point Doubling)

Slope:
$$
\lambda = \frac{3x_1^2 + a}{2y_1}
$$

Coordinates:

$$
x_3 = \lambda^2 - 2x_1
$$

$$
y_3 = \lambda(x_1 - x_3) - y_1
$$

---

## Inverse of Point

$$
P = (x,y)
$$

$$
-P = (x, -y)
$$

---

## Identity Element

$$
\mathcal{O}
$$

* Point at infinity

---

## Group Properties

* Closure

* Associativity

* Identity:
  $$
  P + \mathcal{O} = P
  $$

* Inverse:
  $$
  P + (-P) = \mathcal{O}
  $$

* Commutative:
  $$
  P + Q = Q + P
  $$

✔ Forms **Abelian group**

---

## Key Operation (Scalar Multiplication)

$$
kP = P + P + \cdots + P
$$

---

## Cryptographic Idea

* Given:
  $$
  P,; kP
  $$

❌ Hard to find:
$$
k
$$

✔ Elliptic Curve Discrete Log Problem

---

## Key Takeaways

* Points on curve form group
* Addition defined geometrically + algebraically
* Security based on:
  $$
  \text{ECDLP}
  $$

---

## Final Conclusion

* Elliptic curves provide:

$$
\text{High security with smaller key sizes}
$$

✔ Widely used in modern cryptography (ECC)

## Lecture 30: <br />Elliptic curve Modulo a Prime
:::

## Over ($$\mathbb{Z}_p $$)

---

## Curve

$$
y^2 \equiv x^3 + ax + b \pmod{p}
$$

* Points:
  $$
  (x,y) \in \mathbb{Z}_p
  $$

* Plus:
  $$
  \mathcal{O}
  $$

---

## Finding Points

For each:
$$
x \in {0,1,2,...,p-1}
$$

Compute:
$$
z = x^3 + ax + b \mod p
$$

---

## Check Validity

* Check if $$ ( z ) $$ is quadratic residue:

$$
z^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}
$$

✔ If true → valid point exists

---

## Compute ( y )

If:
$$
p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}
$$

Then:

$$
y = z^{\frac{p+1}{4}} \mod p
$$

* Points:
  $$
  (x, y),; (x, -y)
  $$

---

## Example

Given:
$$
p = 11
$$

$$
y^2 \equiv x^3 + x + 6 \pmod{11}
$$

---

## Quadratic Residues mod 11

$$
{1,3,4,5,9}
$$

---

## Valid Points

$$
(2,4), (2,7)
$$

$$
(3,5), (3,6)
$$

$$
(5,2), (5,9)
$$

$$
(7,2), (7,9)
$$

$$
(8,3), (8,8)
$$

$$
(10,2), (10,9)
$$

---

## Total Points

$$
12 + 1 = 13
$$

(including $$( \mathcal{O} )$$) 

---

## Notes

* For each valid ( x ):

  * Either 0 or 2 points
* Symmetry:
  $$
  y \leftrightarrow -y
  $$

---

## Key Idea

* Points form group under addition
* Used for:
  $$
  \text{Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC)}
  $$
