## Lecture 41: <br />Universal Hashing
:::

## Universal Hashing (Idea)

* Avoid worst-case inputs by **randomly choosing hash function at runtime** 

---

## Definition

A family of hash functions ( $$\mathcal{H}$$ ) is **universal** if:

$$
\Pr_{h \in \mathcal{H} }[h(x) = h(y)] \le \frac{1}{m}, \quad x \ne y
$$

---

## Why Useful

* Prevents adversarial inputs
* Guarantees **low expected collisions**
* Works even if input is malicious

---

## Collision Indicator

Define:

$$
C_{xy} =
\begin{cases}
1 & h(x) = h(y) \\
0 & \text{otherwise}\\
\end{cases}
$$

---

## Expectation

$$
E[C_{xy}] = \Pr[h(x)=h(y)] = \frac{1}{m}
$$

---

## Expected Collisions for Key ($x$)

Let set size = ($n$)

$$
E[\text{collisions with } x] = \frac{n-1}{m} \approx \alpha
$$

(where  $\alpha = \frac{n}{m}$ )

---

## Key Result

$$
E[\text{collisions}] \le \alpha
$$

✔ Small load factor → fewer collisions

---

## Construction of Universal Family

Example form:

$$
h_a(k) = \left( \sum_{i=1}^{r} a_i k_i \right) \mod m
$$

* Parameters:

  * $$(a_i)$$ chosen randomly
  * Each $$(a_i \in { 0,1,\dots,m-1 } )$$

---

## Size of Family

If there are (r) parameters:

$$
|\mathcal{H}| = m^r
$$

---

## Bad Hash Functions

* Cause:
  $$
  h(x) = h(y)
  $$

* Count of such “bad” functions:

$$
\le m^{r-1}
$$

---

## Probability of Bad Choice

$$
\frac{m^{r-1}}{m^r} = \frac{1}{m}
$$

✔ Matches universal condition

---

## Two-Level (Perfect Hashing)

### Idea

* First level:

  * Distribute keys into buckets

* Second level:

  * Build separate hash table for each bucket

---

## Property

For bucket with $n_i$ keys:

$$
\text{Expected collisions} \propto n_i^2
$$

---

## Guarantee

* Total space:
  $$
  O(n)
  $$

* Expected collisions:
  $$
  < \frac{1}{2}
  $$

✔ Can achieve **perfect hashing (no collisions)**

---

## Key Insight

* Randomization removes worst-case
* Universal hashing → expected $O(1)$ operations
* Perfect hashing → collision-free lookup

## Lecture 42: <br />Cryptographic Hash Function
:::

## Hash Function (Cryptographic Requirements)

* Maps arbitrary input → fixed size output

$$
h: {0,1}^* \rightarrow {0,1}^n
$$

---

## Required Properties

### 1. Arbitrary Input Size

* Accept any length message

---

### 2. Fixed Output Size

* Output length is constant

---

### 3. Pre-image Resistance (One-way)

Given:
$$
h(x)
$$

Hard to find:
$$
x
$$

---

### 4. Second Pre-image Resistance

Given:
$$
x
$$

Hard to find:
$$
x' \ne x \text{ such that } h(x') = h(x)
$$

---

### 5. Collision Resistance

Hard to find:
$$
x \ne y \text{ such that } h(x) = h(y)
$$

---

## Use in Authentication

### Basic Method

* Alice sends:
  $$
  (M,; h(M))
  $$

* Bob:

  * Computes:
    $$
    h(M)
    $$
  * Compares

❌ No security (attacker can modify both)

---

## With Secret Key (MAC)

* Shared key:
  $$
  K
  $$

* Alice sends:
  $$
  (M,; h(K || M))
  $$

* Bob verifies:
  $$
  h(K || M)
  $$

✔ Provides authentication

---

## With Encryption

* Alice sends:
  $$
  M || E_K(h(M))
  $$

* Bob:

  * Decrypts:
    $$
    h(M)
    $$
  * Verifies

✔ Authentication + confidentiality

---

## With Digital Signature

* Alice signs:
  $$
  S = E_{d_A}(h(M))
  $$

* Sends:
  $$
  (M,; S)
  $$

* Bob verifies:
  $$
  h(M) = D_{e_A}(S)
  $$

✔ Strong authentication + non-repudiation

---

## Hash Construction (Iterative)

* Message split into blocks:
  $$
  M_1, M_2, ..., M_t
  $$

* Initial value:
  $$
  H_0 = IV
  $$

* Process:

$$
H_i = f(H_{i-1}, M_i)
$$

* Final hash:
  $$
  H_t
  $$

---

## Key Insight

* Hash ensures integrity
* MAC → adds authentication
* Signature → adds identity proof

---

## Final Note

* Secure hash must satisfy:

  * One-way
  * Collision resistant
  * Efficient

✔ Used in all modern cryptographic systems

## Lecture 43: <br />Secure Hash Algorithm (SHA)
:::

## Secure Hash Algorithms

---

## SHA Family

* Developed by **NIST** (1993)
* SHA-1 later replaced older versions

---

## Variants

* SHA-1 → 160-bit
* SHA-256 → 256-bit
* SHA-384 → 384-bit
* SHA-512 → 512-bit

---

## Message Preprocessing (SHA-1)

### Step 1: Padding

* Append:
  $$
  1 \text{ followed by } 0\text{s}
  $$

* So that length ≡ 448 mod 512

---

### Step 2: Append Length

* Add original length (64-bit)

---

### Step 3: Blocks

* Divide message into:
  $$
  512\text{-bit blocks}
  $$

---

## Initialization

* Initial hash values (IV):
  $$
  H_0, H_1, H_2, H_3, H_4
  $$

---

## Processing (Per Block)

* 80 rounds

* Uses:

  * Logical functions
  * Constants
  * Message schedule

---

## Compression

For each block:

$$
CV_{i+1} = \text{Compress}(CV_i, M_i)
$$

---

## Final Hash

$$
\text{Digest} = CV_L
$$

---

## Birthday Attack

* Based on probability of collisions

---

## Probability Idea

* Want:
  $$
  P(\text{collision}) > 0.5
  $$

---

## Approximation

$$
P \approx 1 - e^{-\frac{k^2}{2n}}
$$

---

## Solve for k

$$
\frac{k^2}{2n} \approx \ln 2
$$

$$
k \approx \sqrt{2n \ln 2} \approx \sqrt{n}
$$

---

## Example (Birthday Problem)

* Total days:
  $$
  n = 365
  $$

* Required people:
  $$
  k \approx 23
  $$

✔ >50% chance of same birthday

---

## Implication in Hashing

* For n-bit hash:

$$
\text{Collision effort} \approx 2^{n/2}
$$

---

## Example

* SHA-1 (160-bit):

$$
2^{80} \text{ operations for collision}
$$

---

## Key Insight

* Collisions easier than brute force
* Due to:
  $$
  \text{Birthday paradox}
  $$

---

## Final Notes

* SHA-1 → broken (collision attacks exist)
* Use:

  * SHA-256 or higher

✔ Stronger security against collisions

## Lecture 44: <br />Digital Signature Standard (DSS)
:::

## Parameters

* Primes:
  $$
  p,; q \quad (q \mid p-1)
  $$

* Generator:
  $$
  g = h^{\frac{p-1}{q}} \mod p
  $$

---

## Key Generation

* Private key:
  $$
  x \in {1,2,...,q-1}
  $$

* Public key:
  $$
  y = g^x \mod p
  $$

---

## Signing

* Hash message:
  $$
  H = h(M)
  $$

* Choose random:
  $$
  k \in {1,2,...,q-1}
  $$

---

### Compute

$$
r = (g^k \mod p) \mod q
$$

$$
s = k^{-1}(H + xr) \mod q
$$

---

## Signature

$$
(r, s)
$$

---

## Verification

* Check:
  $$
  0 < r,s < q
  $$

---

### Compute

$$
w = s^{-1} \mod q
$$

$$
u_1 = Hw \mod q
$$

$$
u_2 = rw \mod q
$$

---

### Compute

$$
v = ((g^{u_1} \cdot y^{u_2}) \mod p) \mod q
$$

---

## Validity

$$
v = r \Rightarrow \text{valid}
$$

---

## Correctness

$$
s = k^{-1}(H + xr)
$$

$$
\Rightarrow k = (H + xr)s^{-1}
$$

Substitute in:

$$
g^k = g^{H s^{-1}} \cdot g^{x r s^{-1}}
$$

$$
= g^{u_1} \cdot y^{u_2}
$$

---

## Key Points

* Uses hash:
  $$
  h(M)
  $$

* Random:
  $$
  k \text{ must be secret and unique}
  $$

* Security based on:
  $$
  \text{Discrete Log Problem}
  $$

---

## Important Notes

* Reusing (k) → private key leak
* Requires secure random generation

---

## Final Insight

* Signature = pair:
  $$
  (r,s)
  $$

* Verification uses:

  * Public key
  * Hash
  * Modular arithmetic

✔ Standardized digital signature scheme

## Lecture 45: <br />More on Key Exchange Protocol
:::

## 3-Party Key Exchange (Basic Idea)

* Parties: Alice, Bob, Charlie

* Public:
  $$
  g,; p
  $$

* Secrets:
  $$
  a,; b,; c
  $$

---

## Exchange

* Alice sends:
  $$
  g^a
  $$

* Bob sends:
  $$
  g^b
  $$

* Charlie sends:
  $$
  g^c
  $$

---

## Goal

* Shared key:
  $$
  K = g^{abc} \mod p
  $$

---

## Problem

* With normal exponentiation:

  * Requires multiple rounds
  * Not efficient

---

## Bilinear Pairing Setup

* Groups:
  $$
  G_1 \text{ (additive)}, \quad G_2 \text{ (multiplicative)}
  $$

* Pairing:
  $$
  e: G_1 \times G_1 \rightarrow G_2
  $$

---

## Properties

### 1. Bilinearity

$$
e(aP, bP) = e(P,P)^{ab}
$$

---

### 2. Non-degeneracy

$$
e(P,P) \ne 1
$$

---

### 3. Computability

* Efficient to compute

---

## Public Setup

* Generator:
  $$
  P \in G_1
  $$

* Public:
  $$
  P,; G_1,; G_2,; e
  $$

---

## Private Keys

$$
a,; b,; c
$$

---

## Exchange (1 Round)

* Alice sends:
  $$
  aP
  $$

* Bob sends:
  $$
  bP
  $$

* Charlie sends:
  $$
  cP
  $$

---

## Key Computation

### Alice

$$
K = e(bP, cP)^a = e(P,P)^{abc}
$$

---

### Bob

$$
K = e(aP, cP)^b = e(P,P)^{abc}
$$

---

### Charlie

$$
K = e(aP, bP)^c = e(P,P)^{abc}
$$

---

## Shared Key

$$
K = e(P,P)^{abc}
$$

✔ Same for all three

---

## Key Insight

* Pairing enables:
  $$
  \text{multiplication of exponents across parties}
  $$

* Achieves:

  * 3-party key agreement
  * Single round communication

---

## Notes

* Used in:

  * Identity-based cryptography
  * Advanced protocols

* Still active research area
